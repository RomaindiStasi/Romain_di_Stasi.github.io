About Me

I am a PhD candidate in Comparative Literature and Literary Theory at the University of Pennsylvania, where I also serve as a Graduate Associate at the Alice Paul Center for Research on Gender, Sexuality, and Women. My research focuses on nineteenth-century French and Italian literature, with particular emphasis on gender studies, queer theory, and the intersection of literature with social and political movements.

My dissertation, titled "Clandestine Circulations: Anarchism, Pornography, and Gender in the Franco-Italian Underground, 1870-1914," examines the relationship between anarchist political discourse and pornographic literature in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. This work explores how these seemingly disparate discourses intersected in the underground press and contributed to debates about gender, sexuality, and social transformation.

Beyond my research, I am deeply committed to teaching and have developed courses on French literature, gender studies, and comparative literature. I also engage in various forms of public scholarship and community outreach, believing that academic work should contribute to broader social conversations and positive change.

Current Research Projects

Dissertation: Clandestine Circulations

My dissertation examines the intersection of anarchist political discourse and pornographic literature in the Franco-Italian underground press from 1870 to 1914. This project analyzes how these discourses challenged normative conceptions of gender and sexuality while advancing revolutionary political agendas. Through archival research in France and Italy, I trace the networks of writers, publishers, and distributors who created and circulated these texts, often at great personal risk.

Digital Humanities Project: Mapping the Anarchist Press

In collaboration with colleagues at the Penn Libraries, I am developing a digital mapping project that visualizes the networks of anarchist publications across Europe in the late nineteenth century. This project uses network analysis and geographic visualization to reveal patterns of circulation, influence, and repression.

Book Project: Queer Temporalities in French Decadence

This project explores how French Decadent writers of the fin de siècle imagined alternative temporal frameworks that challenged heteronormative narratives of progress and reproduction. Drawing on queer theory and affect studies, I examine works by authors such as Joris-Karl Huysmans, Jean Lorrain, and Rachilde.
